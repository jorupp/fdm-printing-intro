Introduction to FDM 3D printing

What is FDM printing

  • Fused deposition modeling is an additive manufacturing technology based on using depositing a material onto a platform in lasers and fusing it together to make a product.
  • Essentially, we deposit molten plastic in a series of very thin layers, and as it cools, it hardens into a solid object.

How does it work?

The plastic filament is drawn in from a spool, heated in the hot end, pushed out through an extruder nozzle on to a build plate. The position of the build plate and extruder are driven by a series of motors to achieve motion along the X, Y, and Z axes.

In the Ultimaker 2, the X and Y axes are handled by moving the extruder assembly around the top of the machine, while the Z axis is handled by moving the build plate up and down.

So, what kinds of materials work?

You can't just melt down plastic parts and re-use them (not yet, there are some Kickstarter projects though).

  • PLA: a plant-based plastic, little smell, prints not very flexible. Prints well at 180-220 C, even without a heated bed
  • ABS: a petroleum-based plastic, can smell a bit during printing. Prints something roughly like Lego blocks. Prints in the 220-250 C range, usually needs a heated bed and/or glue or other approaches to stick well.

More exotic approaches

There are some more exotic materials being experiemented with, like metal or wood suspended in plastic.

Resin + laser systems are also getting closer to being available in a desktop form factor and price range.

Where do I build/get designs to print?

  • Thingiverse - large library of existing designs to download
  • Tinkercad - design software so simple a child could use it (which makes it great for design newbies like me).

Really though, any library that has or design tool that can output .STL files will work.

So, once I have an .STL file, what do I do with it?

The printer can't directly use a .STL file - it needs to be 'sliced' and output in a format called GCODE. GCODE is basically a series of instructions to the printer to move the head to a series of specific locations and turn the extruder on and off that should result in your print being created.

This slicing process may add some extra bits to your print to get it to print well:

  • Support: exta bits of plastic underneath your model so your model has something to sit on.
  • Brim: wider base so more of the model touches the bulid plate - useful to make sure your model sticks well and resist falling over.

To slice your design, you want to use a tool like Cura, or one of many other similar tools (though since Cura is from the company that makes the Ultimaker 2, it has a profile for it already).

How do I get the GCODE to the printer?

You can direct-connect to the printer via USB, but then you have to stay connected during the print.

The preferred way is to use an SD card - the printer should have some sitting beside it to use.

Printer mechanics

All the controls are at the bottom-front - SD card slot on the left, screen in the center, control-wheel on the right (rotate and click - just like the old iPods).

Filament is fed into the slot above the arrow at the back-left, and the filment sits on a spool on the back.

Possible issues and solutions

  • First layer doesn't stick: Need more filament or build plate heat, or add glue, tape, or brim. May also need to re-level the build plate.
  • Support doesn't disconnect: configure it to be further away and/or smaller.
  • First layer doesn't stick evenly: Re-level the build plate.

How to level the build plate

Select that operation from the menu, then run though the instructions as-given

  • You'll be asked to position the nozzle just over the build plate in 3 different spots (back-center by moving the wheels, and front-left and front-right by the screws).
  • The best way to get a good distance is to use a normal sheet of paper between the bed and nozzle - it should be as tight as possible, but still move freely.

Rules for use

  • Be reasonable. Remember this is an office - don't print things you'd be embarassed to have seen sitting on your desk in front of Brad, Ross, or a client.
  • Avoid unnecessarily large or detailed models. Depending on the intended quality, consider using the fast mode in Cura (Fast vs. Ulti quality can be a difference in print time of 1 hr vs. 6 hr).
  • Avoid monopolizing the printer.
  • More rules subject to being added in the future.
  • Make sure to return the tools and unused filament to the bins when not in use and close the lid. Moisture degrades the filament.
